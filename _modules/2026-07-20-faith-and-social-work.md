## Part 1: The Anchor (Minutes 1–15)
### Pastoral Cycle: See & Judge | Inductive Method: Observation & Interpretation

### Scripture Reading
> **Nehemiah 5:1–5 (ESV)**
> "Now there arose a great outcry of the people and of their wives against their Jewish brethren. For there were those who said, 'With our sons and our daughters, we are many. So let us get grain, that we may eat and keep alive.' There were also those who said, 'We are mortgaging our fields, our vineyards, and our houses to get grain because of the famine.' And there were those who said, 'We have borrowed money for the king's tax on our fields and our vineyards. Now our flesh is as the flesh of our brothers, our children as their children. Yet we are forcing our sons and our daughters to be slaves, and some of our daughters have already been enslaved, but it is not in our power to help it, for our fields and our vineyards belong to others.'"

### Historical-Cultural Context
*   **Author and Audience:** Written during the post-exilic period, Nehemiah was leading the rebuilding of Jerusalem's physical walls while addressing the internal collapse of the social fabric.
*   **Systemic Environment:** The community was suffering from natural environmental stress (a famine) layered on top of structural economic exploitation. Local wealthy elites used high-interest grain loans and tax burdens to systematically seize family lands and force vulnerable children into debt slavery.
*   **The Broken System:** The governing authorities and wealthy citizens prioritized personal economic gain over the solidarity and survival of their fellow community members.

### Inductive Text Questions
*   **Observation:** Who is making the outcry in this passage, and what specific economic systems (taxes, mortgages, labor exploitation) are driving them to despair?
*   **Interpretation:** How does the loss of land and child slavery violate the theological vision of Holistic Shalom? Why does the text emphasize that the victims' "flesh is as the flesh of our brothers"?

<details>
<summary>Group Leader Guidance Prompts</summary>
*   Encourage the group to notice that the crisis is caused by internal exploitation among neighbors, not external enemies.
*   Highlight how structural poverty strips away a family's capacity for self-determination.
*   Connect the concept of shared brotherhood to the modern pursuit of community-wide economic solidarity.
</details>

## Part 2: The Friction (Minutes 16–30)
### Pastoral Cycle: See & Judge | Inductive Method: Systemic Friction

### The News & The Neighborhood

<details>
<summary>STEEPLE Framework Systems Analysis Checklist</summary>
Use these lenses to understand the professional and structural friction point:
*   **Social:** Navigating personal faith values while respecting client diversity and avoiding paternalism.
*   **Technological:** Keeping client data secure while collaborating with faith networks.
*   **Economic:** Serving people in deep financial crisis within restricted institutional budgets.
*   **Environmental:** Recognizing how resource shortages in neighborhoods drive individual crises.
*   **Political:** Acting as a private citizen for political change while upholding agency guidelines.
*   **Legal:** Compliance with professional licensing boards, state statutes, and employer mandates.
*   **Ethical:** Managing conflicts between religious rules, personal beliefs, and professional standards.
</details>

### Systemic Tension Prompts
*   **The Core Values Meet:** The NASW Code of Ethics highlights core values like Social Justice, Dignity and Worth of the Person, and Integrity. Nehemiah 5 showcases a complete breakdown of these identical values. How does our Christian faith strengthen our commitment to these professional standards?
*   **The Friction Point:** Nehemiah took a public, fiery political stance against the rulers who exploited the poor. For a modern professional bound by code standard 6.04 (Social and Political Action) and standard 1.02 (Self-Determination), how do we navigate taking bold action while protecting the rights and independence of our clients?
*   **Boundary Dynamics:** The NASW Code demands strict boundaries to avoid dual relationships and ensure professional competence. When operating as a person of faith inside local churches or mutual aid groups, how do we honor the professional expertise of social work without turning our private faith spaces into unmanaged clinical settings?
